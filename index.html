⚡ Module 01

Cardiac Electrophysiology

The heart is an electrical machine. Every ECG waveform originates from ion movements across cell membranes.

The Conduction System

Electrical impulses travel through a dedicated highway system in the heart, producing the coordinated contractions we record as an ECG.

⚡

Sinoatrial (SA) Node

Located in the right atrium. The natural pacemaker — fires 60–100 times/min. Initiates every normal heartbeat.

🔵

Atrioventricular (AV) Node

Delays conduction by ~0.12s (the PR interval), allowing atria to fully empty before ventricles contract.

⬇️

Bundle of His

Carries impulse from AV node into the interventricular septum. Divides into Left and Right Bundle Branches.

🌿

Purkinje Fibres

Rapidly distribute the impulse throughout ventricular myocardium → synchronized contraction → QRS complex.

💡

Key concept: The P wave = atrial depolarization. The QRS = ventricular depolarization. The T wave = ventricular repolarization. Atrial repolarization is hidden inside the QRS complex!

📡 Module 03

The 12-Lead ECG

Each lead is a unique electrical viewpoint of the heart. Together, they create a 3D picture of cardiac activity.

Einthoven's Triangle & Limb Leads

The three standard limb leads form an equilateral triangle around the heart. Each measures electrical potential between two points.

I

Lead I — LA(+) vs RA(−)

Horizontal lead. Positive deflection = impulse moving left.

II

Lead II — LL(+) vs RA(−)

Parallels the direction of normal cardiac axis. Usually tallest P & R waves. Best for rhythm strips.

III

Lead III — LL(+) vs LA(−)

Views inferior wall. Used alongside aVF for inferior MI detection.

aVF

aVR / aVL / aVF

Augmented leads — each uses one positive electrode against the average of the other two.

Precordial (Chest) Leads V1–V6

Placed across the chest, these leads view the heart in the horizontal plane and are essential for identifying bundle branch blocks, hypertrophy, and anterior/lateral MI.

〰️ Module 04

Waves, Segments & Intervals

Every deflection on an ECG has a name, a normal value, and a clinical meaning. Let's dissect the waveform.

The Complete PQRST Complex

P

P Wave — Atrial Depolarization

Represents SA node firing → atrial depolarization. Normally smooth and rounded.

Normal: <0.12s duration, <2.5mm tall, upright in I, II
Q

Q Wave — Septal Depolarization

Small initial septal depolarization (left→right). Septal Q waves are normal in I, aVL, V5, V6.

Pathological: >0.04s OR >25% of R height
R

R Wave — Ventricular Depolarization

Main upward deflection. Should progressively increase from V1 to V5 (R-wave progression).

Poor R progression → anterior MI or LBBB
S

S Wave — Basal Depolarization

Downward deflection after R. Represents depolarization of basal ventricular myocardium.

Deep S in V1–V3 → RVH pattern
T

T Wave — Ventricular Repolarization

Upright in most leads. Inverted T in V1–V3 can be normal; elsewhere suggests ischemia.

Peaked T: hyperkalemia. Flat T: hypokalemia, ischemia
U

U Wave — Purkinje Repolarization

Small wave after T, best seen in V2–V3. Often prominent in hypokalemia.

Prominent U: hypokalemia, bradycardia, drugs

🔢 Module 05

Calculating Heart Rate

Three reliable methods — know them all, use the one that fits the situation.

Three Methods to Calculate Rate

Paper speed is 25mm/s. One large box = 0.2s. Use these methods depending on rhythm regularity.

01

The 300 Method

Best for regular rhythms. Count large boxes between two consecutive R waves. Divide 300 by that number.

HR = 300 ÷ (large boxes between R–R)
Memorize: 300, 150, 100, 75, 60, 50
(1 box, 2 boxes, 3, 4, 5, 6 boxes)
02

The 1500 Method

Precise. Count small boxes between two R waves. Divide 1500 by that number.

HR = 1500 ÷ (small boxes between R–R)
Most accurate for regular rhythms. Good for exam calculations.
03

The 6-Second Strip

Best for irregular rhythms (AF). Count R waves in 30 large boxes (6 seconds), multiply by 10.

HR = (QRS complexes in 6s) × 10
Most rhythm strips are 10 seconds long — count in 6s then multiply.

Cardiac Axis Determination

The cardiac axis describes the average direction of ventricular depolarization. Determined mainly from Lead I and aVF.

Normal Axis: −30° to +90°

Lead I positive + aVF positive → normal. Most common finding.

Left Axis Deviation (LAD): −30° to −90°

Lead I positive, aVF negative. Causes: LBBB, LVH, inferior MI, LAFB.

Right Axis Deviation (RAD): +90° to +180°

Lead I negative, aVF positive. Causes: RVH, RBBB, lateral MI, pulmonary embolism.

Extreme Axis: −90° to ±180°

Both Lead I and aVF negative. Rare. Seen in severe RVH, ventricular tachycardia.

💡

Quick trick: If Lead I and aVF are both positive → normal axis. If Lead I is positive and aVF is negative → LAD. Simple!

💓 Module 07

Cardiac Rhythms & Arrhythmias

Arrhythmias range from benign to immediately life-threatening. Recognition is the first step to management.

Normal Sinus Rhythm

Normal
Rate 60–100 bpm. Regular. P before every QRS. PR interval normal. QRS narrow.

Atrial Fibrillation (AF)

Common
Irregularly irregular. No distinct P waves — replaced by fibrillatory baseline. Variable QRS rate 100–160 bpm (untreated).

Supraventricular Tachycardia (SVT)

Paroxysmal
Rate 150–250 bpm. Regular. Narrow QRS. P waves hidden in or after QRS. Abrupt onset/offset. Usually AVNRT or AVRT.

Ventricular Tachycardia (VT)

Dangerous
Rate >100 bpm. Wide QRS (>0.12s). Dissociated P waves. AV dissociation. Haemodynamically unstable — treat urgently!

1° Heart Block

Benign
PR interval >0.20s (one large box). Every P followed by QRS. Regular rhythm. Usually benign — no treatment needed.

2° Heart Block — Mobitz II

Risk of 3°
Fixed PR interval but occasional P wave NOT followed by QRS. Risk of complete heart block. May need pacing.
⚠️

Clinical pearl: Wenckebach (Mobitz I) = PR progressively lengthens then drops a beat. Mobitz II = fixed PR, sudden dropped beat. Mobitz II is more dangerous and often progresses to complete (3°) heart block.

Myocardial Infarction on ECG

The ECG can pinpoint the location of infarction based on which leads show ST elevation — and which show reciprocal changes.

Stages of STEMI Evolution

MI changes evolve over hours to days. Recognizing the stage helps determine timing of infarction.

Inferior MI

Usually RCA (Right Coronary Artery)

Leads: II, III, aVF
Reciprocal: aVL, I
ST elevation II, III, aVF
ST depression aVL (reciprocal)
Q waves II, III, aVF (old)
Check V3R/V4R for RV involvement

Anterior MI

Usually LAD (Left Anterior Descending)

Leads: V1–V4
Reciprocal: II, III, aVF
ST elevation V1–V4
Poor R wave progression
Pathological Q in V1–V4
Most dangerous — large territory

Lateral MI

Usually LCx (Left Circumflex)

Leads: I, aVL, V5, V6
Reciprocal: III, aVF
ST elevation I, aVL, V5–V6
ST depression III, aVF (reciprocal)
Q waves in lateral leads
Often silent / missed
ℹ️

STEMI criteria: ST elevation ≥1mm in ≥2 contiguous limb leads OR ≥2mm in ≥2 contiguous precordial leads. New LBBB may also indicate STEMI. Always compare with old ECGs if available.

📖 Reference

ECG Glossary

Quick definitions for key ECG terms and abbreviations.

Depolarization

The reversal of resting membrane potential due to ion influx — triggers contraction.

Repolarization

Return of the cell to resting potential — corresponds to relaxation (T wave).

Isoelectric line

The flat baseline on ECG; represents no net electrical activity (TP segment).

Sinus Rhythm

Normal rhythm originating from SA node. Rate 60–100 bpm.

AV Block

Impaired conduction through the AV node — classified as 1°, 2° (I & II), or 3°.

STEMI

ST-Elevation Myocardial Infarction — complete occlusion of a coronary artery.

NSTEMI

Non-ST-Elevation MI — partial occlusion; ST depression or T-wave changes.

QTc

QT interval corrected for heart rate using Bazett's formula: QTc = QT ÷ √RR

Delta wave

Slurred upstroke of QRS in Wolff-Parkinson-White — due to pre-excitation via accessory pathway.

LBBB

Left Bundle Branch Block. QRS >0.12s. Broad notched R in V6. William pattern.

RBBB

Right Bundle Branch Block. RSR' in V1 ("M" shape), broad S in V6. Marrow pattern.

Torsades de Pointes

Polymorphic VT with twisting QRS axis — associated with long QT syndrome.

Reciprocal changes

ST depression in leads opposite to the infarct zone — confirms ST elevation is true MI.

Wandering pacemaker

Gradual shift of pacemaker between SA node, atria, and AV junction — variable P morphology.

R-wave progression

Normal increase in R wave amplitude from V1→V5. Loss suggests anterior MI or LVH.

✅ Module 10

A Systematic Approach to Every ECG

Never miss a finding again. Follow this step-by-step framework for every single ECG you read.

1

Rate

Calculate ventricular rate. Is it 60–100? Fast? Slow?

2

Rhythm

Regular or irregular? P waves present? P before every QRS?

3

Axis

Check Lead I and aVF. Normal / LAD / RAD / extreme?

4

P Wave

Shape, duration (<0.12s), height (<2.5mm), axis (upright in I, II)?

5

PR Interval

0.12–0.20s? Long = heart block. Short = WPW/LGL.

6

QRS Complex

Width (<0.12s), morphology (BBB?), Q waves (pathological?), R progression.

7

ST Segment & T Wave

Elevation/depression? T wave inversion? Hyperacute T? Which leads?

8

QT Interval & Conclusion

Measure QTc. Summarize all findings. Correlate with clinical context.
